Jungles are typical for monsoon areas of the tropics. The largest jungles exist in the Amazon river basin (Amazon Rain forests), in Nicaragua, in the southern part of the Yucatan Peninsula (Guatemala, Belize), in most of Central America (where they are called "Selva"), in Equatorial Africa from Cameroon to the Democratic Republic of the Congo, in many areas of Southeast Asia from Myanmar to Indonesia and Papua New Guinea, in the Australian state of Queensland.
Trees in the jungle have several common characteristics that are not observed in plants in less humid climates. The base of the trunk in many species has wide, woody projections. Previously, it was assumed that these protrusions help the tree to keep its balance, but now it is believed that water with dissolved nutrients flows down these protrusions to the roots of the tree. Broad leaves are also common in trees, shrubs, and grasses in the lower tiers of the forest. Tall young trees that have not yet reached the top tier also have broader foliage, which then decreases with height. Broad leaves help plants better absorb sunlight under the edges of forest trees, and they are protected from the wind from above. The leaves of the upper tier that form the canopy are usually smaller and heavily cut to reduce wind pressure. On the lower floors, the leaves are often narrowed at the ends so that this contributes to the rapid flow of water and prevents the proliferation of microbes and moss that destroy the leaves. The tops of trees are often very well connected with each other by means of lianas or epiphyte plants that attach themselves to them. Other characteristics of the jungle can serve as an unusually thin (1-2 mm) tree bark, sometimes covered with sharp thorns or spines; the presence of flowers and fruits growing directly on the trunks of trees; a large variety of juicy fruits that attract birds, mammals, and even fish that feed on dispersed particles.
